01 / Executive Summary

Executive Summary

Koaryu is a flat-rate, vertical SaaS platform purpose-built for independent martial arts studios with 30 to 200 active students. It replaces the three tools that dojo owners currently patch together: expensive legacy software like RainMaker, now rebranded as Zivvy and commonly sold at $197 per month (Software Advice, RainMaker pricing); generic gym platforms such as Mindbody that start at $139 per month and scale to $599 per month before add-ons (The Salon Business, Mindbody Review); and a long tail of Google Sheets, Notion docs, and handwritten belt ladders that quietly run the majority of schools in the country.

The core thesis is simple. Martial arts studios have domain-specific operational needs, especially around belt progression, attendance-driven testing readiness, youth guardian records, and promotion history, that horizontal fitness software consistently handles poorly. At the same time, the vertical platforms that do understand those workflows have priced themselves out of reach for the typical owner-operator. Koaryu is positioned precisely in that gap: deep domain fit, flat $27/month pricing that does not punish growth, embedded Stripe Connect payments that let studios collect tuition without a second payment vendor, and a UI that feels like a modern developer tool rather than a legacy admin panel.

Since the previous pitch revision in April 2026, the product has shipped a complete Stripe Connect billing layer marketed as Koaryu Payments, family payer and plan-enrollment data model, first-class programs with per-program belt ladders, staff invites with admin / front desk / instructor roles, light and dark themes, custom error pages, a public landing page, and a tightened single-Uvicorn Render backend deploy. The repository now contains roughly 17,500 lines of Python across 17 services and 16 endpoint groups, 24,500 lines of TypeScript across the frontend, and 29 sequential Supabase migrations applied in order.

02 / The Problem

The problem we are solving

Independent martial arts studios are small businesses with big operational complexity. A typical owner-operator teaches classes, runs billing, answers parent emails, tracks belt progression across several programs, and still has to decide every few weeks who is ready to test. The software market has given them two bad options and no good one.

Option one: expensive legacy vertical tools

Tools like RainMaker (now Zivvy), Zen Planner, and MyStudio understand belt tracking, but they price like enterprise software. RainMaker is listed at $197 per month or $2,197 per year (Angel Rated, Rainmaker Customer Reviews). Zen Planner starts at $99 and commonly lands near $169 per month for a 150-student martial arts school with rank tracking (Zen Planner, Pricing Breakdown). MyStudio lists $79, $159, and $239 plans (MyStudio, Single Location Pricing). Spark Membership publishes a $249 per month price after an introductory trial (Spark Membership, Pricing). Several of these platforms also meter based on student count, so software gets more expensive precisely when the school is growing.

Option two: generic gym platforms

Mindbody, Gymdesk, and similar platforms are broad and capable but miss martial-arts-native workflows. Mindbody’s Starter plan begins at $139 per month, Accelerate at $289, and Ultimate Plus at $599 (The Salon Business, Mindbody Review). Gymdesk scales from $75 to $200 per month strictly by member count and bolts on SMS and payment fees on top (Wodify, Gymdesk Pricing). None of these treat a belt ladder, a tip, or a promotion ceremony as a first-class database object. Owners end up maintaining a side spreadsheet just to decide who tests next month.

Option three, the silent default: spreadsheets

A large share of U.S. studios still run on Google Sheets, paper rosters, and memory. There are roughly 15,900 to 28,530 active martial arts schools in the country depending on the counting methodology (Kenko, Martial Arts Industry Stats; ByteScraper, List of U.S. Martial Arts Schools), and IBISWorld counts 72,029 establishments including single-instructor studios and micro-schools (IBISWorld, Number of Businesses). Of those, only 20,718 have websites at all (ByteScraper). This is a market with a long tail of extremely small operators for whom a $49 product that replaces a spreadsheet is more valuable than a $199 product that replaces another $199 product.

The consequences of bad software

These three failure modes produce concrete, measurable pain.

  • Retention leakage. About 50% of new martial arts members quit within the first six months (Member Solutions, Client Retention). Healthy annual retention is 65% to 75% (Black Belt CRM, Benchmark Report 2026). Attendance signals that predict churn are trapped in spreadsheets that no one reviews.
  • Promotion bottlenecks. Instructors spend hours reconciling class attendance against rank thresholds before a belt test. The industry knows this is a solved problem on paper but unsolved in software that real studios can afford.
  • Revenue drag from failed payments. Without webhook-driven dunning, failed Stripe charges sit as unresolved line items for days or weeks, which SMBs with 3% to 7% monthly SaaS churn benchmarks (HubiFi, SaaS Churn Rates) cannot afford.
  • Switching cost. Legacy tools are painful to migrate off because exports are incomplete and CSV imports in competitors are famously fragile. The tax is real; the pain is compounded by long contracts.
Stated plainly. The average small martial arts studio pays too much for software it does not fully use, or it pays nothing and runs on spreadsheets that quietly lose it students. Koaryu closes that gap with a product priced for the market that actually exists.

Who the early customer actually is

The primary Koaryu customer is an owner-operator running a single-location karate, taekwondo, BJJ, or hybrid family martial arts studio with 30 to 200 active students. They teach most classes themselves or alongside one or two instructors, handle parent communication directly, and are highly sensitive to software cost because tuition margins are already tight. They usually own their email list but not their operational data. Their current stack is one of:

  • A paid legacy tool they consider overpriced and overbuilt (RainMaker / Zivvy, Mindbody, Zen Planner).
  • A flat-rate competitor with weaker belt tracking or weaker CRM (Kicksite, Spark, Gymdesk).
  • A Google Sheet, Notion database, or paper roster that loses critical retention signals every week.

Why this customer will switch

Three reasons, in decreasing order: price, belt tracking that actually matches how they run tests, and setup fast enough that they can be operational the same day. Studios are not asking for a seventh dashboard. They are asking for the first one that feels like it was built by someone who has stood on a mat and run a belt test.

04 / Product

Product

Koaryu ships nine integrated modules in the current build. They cover the overwhelming majority of daily studio operations and are designed as deterministic, database-backed workflows rather than LLM-dependent runtime features. This matters for cost, reliability, and trust with studios that handle minors’ data.

1. Student CRM

Canonical student profile with legal name, preferred name, date of birth, guardian fields for minors, membership status, program memberships, current belt rank, tags, attendance history, payment status, belt history, student photos, and promotion history in one place. Search, filter, bulk actions, CSV export, soft-delete for auditability.

2. Belt progression engine

Configurable belt ladders with ordered ranks, color metadata, tips, minimum classes, minimum months at current rank, optional age brackets, and manual instructor approval flags. Ladders are program-aware: a school running a Tiny Tigers ladder alongside an Adult Black Belt ladder gets independent rank sets. Promotion readiness is computed from actual attendance via a set-based query. Promotions write to an immutable log with prior rank, new rank, date, and staff actor.

3. Programs as first-class objects

Every student belongs to one or more programs; every class, lead, and billing plan ties back to a program. The system seeds an “Unassigned” program automatically so legacy data is never orphaned, and integrity triggers prevent cross-program attendance from being silently lost. Reporting and pipelines roll up cleanly by program without manual tag hygiene.

4. Lead pipeline

Visual pipeline with Inquiry, Trial Scheduled, Trial Completed, Offer Sent, Enrolled, and Closed Lost stages. Guardian-aware intake for youth leads. Source attribution for referral, social, search, and walk-in. Closed-lost reason codes. Conversion metrics by source, stage, and program.

5. Scheduling and attendance

Weekly recurring classes with soft-delete and series semantics, one-off events, instructor assignment, capacity caps, program labels. Mobile-friendly roster with sub-10-second check-in. Bulk attendance reads collapse a schedule range into a single query rather than per-session fan-out. Attendance feeds promotion readiness, inactivity alerts, class utilization, and profile history.

6. Koaryu Payments · Stripe Connect

Full embedded billing on Stripe Connect, with Accounts v2 (2026-03-25.dahlia) preferred and Express fallback. Studios onboard their own Stripe account from inside Koaryu, then accept tuition with hosted invoices, autopay, and external-payment recording. Family payers, plans tied to programs, and student enrollments form a clean hierarchy. Refunds, disputes, and adjustments are first-class. Webhook signatures are verified separately for platform and Connect events with idempotent projection.

7. Staff roles and invites

Role-based access for admin, front desk, and instructor accounts with email invites. Recursion-safe staff_roles policies. Audit log captures every billing action with actor identity, entity, and metadata for compliance and forensics.

8. Communications and automations

Rules-based email automations via Resend with monthly per-studio quota tracking baked into the schema (email_usage_events). Triggers include trial reminder, missed-class nudge, payment failed, payment recovered, membership ending soon, belt test announcement, and promotion congratulations. Every automation toggleable by studio admins. Templated merge fields, no LLM-generated body text in the critical path.

9. Reports and exports

Dashboard insights and printable reports across roster, retention risk, billing status, attendance trends, and the upcoming belt test list. Export jobs are tracked through their own table and produce CSVs for accountants, instructors, and studio audits.

Tenant model and security

Studio membership is the tenant boundary. Every row containing customer data carries a studio_id. Backend services and RLS policies enforce scope. The live frontend onboarding gate relies on the backend auth profile rather than a fragile direct staff_roles query, after a real recursion bug surfaced during verification and was patched across migrations 20260421000007 and 20260421000008. Repair migrations 20260421000013 through 20260421000015 harden the atomic belt-ladder sync RPC. Migration 20260427000026 moves RLS helper functions out of the public schema entirely and pins search_path on every security-definer function, closing a class of search-path attack. Migration 20260427000027 wraps auth.uid() in subqueries to avoid per-row re-evaluation under large policy scans, which is a common Supabase performance footgun. Validation triggers across the billing schema enforce that every payer, plan, enrollment, invoice, payment, and adjustment belongs to the same studio.

Stripe Connect data model

Koaryu Payments is layered on top of Stripe Connect. A studio onboards a connected account via Accounts v2 with automatic fallback to Express. Local tables form a clean hierarchy: studio_payment_accounts records the connected-account state (charges enabled, payouts enabled, requirements due, platform fee in basis points). billing_payers hold a family payer with a Stripe customer ID. billing_plans tie tuition product offers to one or more programs; billing_plan_prices records each historical Stripe price ID so a plan can be edited without breaking existing subscriptions. student_billing_enrollments link a student, a plan, a payer, and a Stripe subscription item; siblings on the same plan can share a subscription item under migration 20260428000029. billing_invoices, billing_payments, billing_refunds, and billing_disputes mirror Stripe state. Every Stripe event lands in stripe_events with idempotent processing status, so retries and out-of-order webhooks are safe.

Design system

The UI is dark-mode first, minimal, utilitarian, and information-dense. It is designed to look like a tool a serious operator would leave open all day, not a marketing microsite. Inter for all body text, JetBrains Mono for IDs, currency values, and metadata. 6px border radius. No shadows. One warm gold accent (#D6B25E) against a restrained neutral palette. This document you are reading is a direct reflection of that visual system.

What is intentionally out of scope in v1

Point-of-sale hardware, advanced e-commerce, franchise-wide multi-location management, full accounting, a native mobile app, and AI chat. These are not rejections; they are deferred. Every one of them adds implementation and support burden without strengthening the initial wedge, which is a single-location studio replacing a spreadsheet or a $197 tool with something cheaper and tighter.

06 / Business Model

Business model and pricing

Koaryu launches on a single flat-rate platform plan plus an embedded Stripe Connect tuition rail. Pricing is deliberately calibrated to sit far below every vertical incumbent (RainMaker $197, Mindbody $139, MyStudio $79, Kicksite $49 entry) and to fully decouple software cost from student count. The second revenue stream is Koaryu Payments, a 50 basis point platform fee on tuition collected through Stripe Connect.

BILLING_PLATFORM_FEE_BPS=50 is the live configuration variable in backend/app/services/billing_service.py. Standard Stripe processing fees (2.9% plus 30 cents in the US) are a pass-through cost borne by the studio, identical to what a direct Stripe integration would charge.

Why a single flat platform price, not per-student tiers

Per-student pricing is the single most frequently cited frustration with current vertical tools (Capterra Reviews of RainMaker; JoinIt on martial arts membership software). It makes software a tax on growth at exactly the moment a studio needs to reinvest. Koaryu collapses platform pricing down to one number that a 30-student studio and a 300-student studio both pay the same. The Payments take grows naturally with the studio, but it is tied to tuition processed, not seat count, and only applies when the studio chooses to run tuition through Koaryu.

Why a 50 bps Payments take

Koaryu Payments is priced like a piece of infrastructure, not a margin tower. At 50 basis points on a studio that processes $10,500 of tuition per month (75 active students at $140 ARPU per Black Belt CRM benchmarks), Koaryu earns approximately $52.50 per month on top of the $27 platform fee. The total cost to the studio is approximately $79.50 per month for both software and tuition rail combined, which is still below the Mindbody Starter plan alone and roughly half of RainMaker.

Expected ARPU

Year 1 blended ARPU is modeled at approximately $48 per paying studio per month: $27 platform fee plus roughly $21 in Payments fees. The Payments component assumes only 60% Connect adoption among paying studios in Year 1 and an average $7,000 of monthly tuition processed per adopting studio. Adoption climbs to roughly 85% by the end of Year 2 as Koaryu Payments becomes the default path for new signups, lifting blended ARPU to roughly $66.

Future expansion surfaces

  • Multi-location add-on: $15/mo per additional location, unlocking franchise and multi-campus studios.
  • Branded student PWA: $25/mo add-on, modeled after MyStudio and Mindbody add-ons but priced lower.
  • SMS credits: passthrough plus small margin, metered per message.
  • Higher-volume Payments tier: studios processing more than $50k/month in tuition can negotiate to 30 bps, mirroring mature embedded-finance pricing curves.
07 / Go-To-Market

Go-to-market strategy

Wedge: the spreadsheet school

The most efficient initial GTM is direct outreach to studios that are currently not paying for vertical software at all. There are at least 7,812 U.S. schools without a website (ByteScraper) and likely a multiple of that running on paper or spreadsheets inside studios that do have a website. These operators have no switching-cost inertia and no contract to break. The pitch is not “switch from RainMaker”; it is “replace your spreadsheet, get a clean attendance dashboard and belt ladder, and run tuition through one tool, for $27 a month after a 30-day free trial.”

Three-channel launch plan

Channel 1 · Founder-led outreach

Direct email and phone to 500 independent studios in the Midwest, leveraging the founder’s prior work experience inside a martial arts studio. Goal for Month 1–3: 15 design-partner studios on the standard $27 plan after the 30-day trial, with full roadmap input and waived Payments fees for their first 90 days on Connect.

Channel 2 · Community proof

Dojo-owner Facebook groups, MartialTalk, r/martialarts, and regional instructor associations. Share case studies, teardown videos of competitor pricing, and open office hours. Trust compounds in this community; the ROI on visibility is high.

Channel 3 · Content moat

Long-form educational content on attendance-driven promotion workflows, retention benchmarks, and CSV migration guides from every major competitor. SEO targets include “RainMaker alternative”, “Zen Planner pricing”, “martial arts belt tracking software”, “martial arts CRM for spreadsheets”.

Channel 4 · Referrals

One free month for each paid referred studio. Owner-operators talk to each other constantly. Flat-rate pricing makes referral math trivial to explain and removes the fear of a bait-and-switch quote later.

Positioning and messaging

Three outcomes, in order, form the entire messaging framework: lower monthly software cost, simpler daily operations, and built-in belt progression workflows. Anything that does not directly support one of those three messages gets cut from landing pages, emails, and sales calls.

Migration playbook

CSV-first migration is a first-class product feature, not a services offering. Studios can map spreadsheet columns to Koaryu fields in a dedicated import wizard, commit partial records, and clean up missing fields later. A clear on-ramp from the two most common spreadsheet formats (student roster tab plus attendance tab) is the single highest-leverage GTM investment short of paid ads.

11 / Risks

Risks and mitigations

RiskLikelihoodMitigation
Incumbent price drop (RainMaker or Zen Planner introduces a $49 plan) Medium Koaryu’s moat is not price alone; it is also UX density, setup speed, and flat-rate guarantees. A competitor price cut still leaves their UI and onboarding intact.
Slow organic growth in a non-tech-native buyer market Medium Founder-led sales in Months 1–6 does not assume content SEO or viral loops. Every early studio is hand-recruited through direct outreach and community presence.
Supabase pricing or platform risk Low Architecture is portable: schema is vanilla Postgres, auth can migrate to Clerk or Auth0, RLS policies are standard Postgres.
Customer support load outpacing a small team Medium Self-serve onboarding is explicitly a v1 product principle. Help docs, in-app empty states, and CSV import wizard are built to minimize ticket volume.
Legal exposure around minor records and parent data Medium RLS-enforced tenant isolation, rate-limited auth, Stripe-hosted card data, PII export and deletion workflows planned pre-launch.
Churn higher than modeled Medium Koaryu itself reduces the dominant churn driver: missed retention signals. Even at 7% monthly SMB churn (Churnfree), LTV still covers modeled CAC with margin.
Scope creep into generic gym features High if unmanaged The v1 exclusions list (POS, franchise, mobile app, AI chat) is treated as a commitment, not a preference. New surface area must pass a “does this help a single-location martial arts studio before everything else?” test.

What would kill the business

The single most dangerous failure mode is trying to be everything at once. A version-one Koaryu that tries to compete on franchise management, branded mobile apps, SMS marketing, and an AI agent simultaneously will ship none of them well and lose against focused incumbents on each axis. The antidote is ruthless sequencing: win the single-location belt-tracking wedge first, then expand on real customer pull, not investor-deck speculation.

Why now

Three converging forces make this the right moment. First, the U.S. martial arts studios industry grew at 15.3% CAGR in establishment count from 2021 to 2026 as participation rebounded post-pandemic (IBISWorld). Second, the incumbent price umbrella remains structurally elevated, with RainMaker still selling at $197 and Mindbody pushing new customers to $139 floors (Software Advice; The Salon Business). Third, vertical SaaS as a category is compounding at 16.3% globally and 12.9% in the U.S. (Business Research Insights; Vertical Software Market Report). The window to take share in this specific vertical with this specific wedge, founder-market fit plus aggressive flat pricing plus deep domain modeling, is open now and will not be as open in three years after someone else has already taken it.

Closing statement. Koaryu is a calm, focused bet on a durable truth: independent martial arts studios deserve software that matches how they actually run, priced in a way that does not punish their growth, built by someone who has stood on the mat. The prototype exists. The market is large, fragmented, and underserved. The economics are clean. The next twelve months turn it into a real business.
